# Question 1
the domain traces out the first quarter of the unit circle giving us nice bounds
as $x$ goes from $0 \to 1$ $y$ traces out $y = \sqrt{1-x^2}$ making this $y$ supported thus
$$\iint\limits_D\frac{4y^3}{(x+1)^2}dA =\int_0^1\int_{0}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\frac{4y^3}{(x+1)^2}dydx$$
$$\int_{0}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\frac{4y^3}{(x+1)^2}dy = \left. \frac{y^4}{(x+1)^2} \right|^{\sqrt{1-x^2}}_0 = \frac{(1-x^2)^2}{(x+1)^2}$$
$$\int_0^1\frac{(1-x^2)^2}{(x+1)^2}dx = \int_0^1\frac{(1-x)^2(1+x)^2}{(x+1)^2}dx = \int_0^1(1-x)^2dx =\left. \frac{-(1-x)^3}{3}\right|^1_0 = \frac{1}{3}$$
$$\iint\limits_D\frac{4y^3}{(x+1)^2}dA = \frac13$$
{{ENDQUESTION}}
# Question 2
line intersects at
$-x = 2x \implies (0,0)$
$2x = 5 \implies (2.5, 5)$
$-x = 5 \implies (-5,5)$
convert from $y$ to $x$ to avoid computing the inner integral in $y$
$x = \frac y2$
$x = -y$
$$\iint\limits_T\cos(y^2)dy = \int_{0}^5\int_{-y}^{\frac y2}\cos(y^2)dxdy$$
$$\int_{0}^5\left. x\cos(y^2)\right|^{\frac y2}_{-y} dy = \int_{0}^5 (\frac y2)\cos(y^2) + y\cos(y^2) dy = \frac32\int_{0}^5 y\cos(y^2)dy$$
$$\frac32\int_{0}^5 y\cos(y^2)dy = \left. 3\sin(y^2)\right|^5_0 = 3\sin(25)$$
$$\iint\limits_T\cos(y^2)dy = 3\sin(25)$$
{{ENDQUESTION}}

# Question 4
this region essentially traces out the arc of a circle with $r = 9$ where the angle is $45^{\circ}$ or $\pi/4$ from the x axis
so obviously convert to polar knowing that 
$x^2+y^2 = r^2$
$r^2 \leq 9 \implies 0 \leq r \leq 3$
$0 \leq \theta \leq \pi/4$
$$\iint\limits_D\sqrt{1+4(x^2+y^2)}dA = \int_0^{\pi/4}\int_0^3 r\sqrt{1+4r^2}drd\theta$$
$$\int_0^3 r\sqrt{1+4r^2}dr = \left. \frac1{12} (\sqrt{1+4r^2})^3\right|^3_0 = \frac1{12}(37\sqrt{37} -1)$$
$$\int_0^{\pi/4}\frac1{12}(37\sqrt{37} -1)d\theta = \left. \frac1{12}(37\sqrt{37} -1)\theta\right|^{\pi/4}_0 = \frac{\pi}{48}(37\sqrt{37} -1)$$
$$\iint\limits_D\sqrt{1+4x^2+4y^2}dA = \frac{\pi}{48}(37\sqrt{37} -1)$$

# Question 5


$z=y-4$ is the bottom and $z = 8-x$ is the top so the volume is just 
$$\iint_\limits{R}(\text{top}-\text{bottom})dA = \iint_\limits{R}((8-x)-(y-4))dA = \iint_\limits{R}12-x-ydA$$
where $R$ is 
$$R = \{(x,y) | x^2+2y^2 \leq 8\}$$
rewriting in standard ellipse form
$\frac{x^2}{8} + \frac{y^2}{4} = 1$
shows us that $y$ varies between $\pm 2$
and solving for $x$ gives us
$x = \pm\sqrt{8-2y^2}$
so we can rewrite our region as an iterated integral

$$V = \iint_\limits{R}12-x-ydA = \int^2_{-2}\int_{-\sqrt{8-2y^2}}^{\sqrt{8-2y^2}}(12-x-y)dxdy$$
$$\int^2_{-2}\int_{-\sqrt{8-2y^2}}^{\sqrt{8-2y^2}}(12-x-y)dxdy = \int^2_{-2}(24-2y)\sqrt{8-2y^2}dy$$
$$\int^2_{-2}(24-2y)\sqrt{8-2y^2}dy = 24\int^2_{-2}\sqrt{8-2y^2}dy-2\int^2_{-2}y\sqrt{8-2y^2}dy$$
2nd integral is even over a symmetric region so its $0$
$$\int^2_{-2}(24-2y)\sqrt{8-2y^2}dy = 24\int^2_{-2}\sqrt{8-2y^2}dy = 24\sqrt{2}\int^2_{-2}\sqrt{4-y^2}dy$$
use the fact that the integral is basically outlining a semicircle with $r = 2$ 
$$V = 24\sqrt2(2\pi) =48\pi\sqrt2$$
